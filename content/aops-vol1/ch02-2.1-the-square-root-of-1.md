The Square Root of -1

The study of complex numbers begins when we are bold enough to ask a very childish question: what is the square root of - 1? Forbidden by sixth grade teachers the world over, the expression V -l is nevertheless the key to a whole branch of math. Historically, people were led to write V -l by the quest to solve equations. Clearly, an equation like x2 + 1 = 0 (2 .1) has only the solutions x = ± V -l. In order to be able to solve all equations, it was decided to accept V -l as a legitimate number. The square root of - 1 is usually written as i. This weird number shows its weird properties almost from the beginning, as we shall see. It is not a real number in the mathematical sense. This is not to say it is not real, at least any less than negatives are. If we multiply i by a real number like 2 or 7i, we get a number like 2i or nr, there is no way to simplify this product. Numbers like this, formed by multiplying i by a real, are called pure imaginary numbers, though you should not let this prejudice of name keep you from accepting them as regular numbers. Treat the word imaginary as a purely mathematical definition. If we multiply i by itself, we get V -l V -l = ( V - l )2 = - 1, as we would expect. But notice that if we try to combine the radicals and write V -l V -l = \/(-l)2 = 1, we will get the wrong answer. Manipulations like this are forbidden. If we keep taking powers of i we get z3 = ii2 = - i, i4 = ii3 = i(-i) = - i 2 = 1, i5 = ii4 = i, z6 = - 1, etc. The powers of i go in cycles of 4: i, - 1, - i, 1, i, - 1, - i, 1, etc. EXERCISE 2-1 What is z17?
