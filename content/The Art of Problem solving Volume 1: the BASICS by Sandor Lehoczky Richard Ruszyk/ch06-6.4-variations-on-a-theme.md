Variations on a Theme

Not all quadratic equations look like ax2 + bx + c = 0. In this section, we will work a series of examples to show you other forms of quadratic equations and how to solve them. These can generally be divided into two categories, rearrangement problems and substitution problems. The former can be solved by manipulating the equation into quadratic form through multiplications and other operations; the latter can be made into a quadratic with a clever substitution. Although these substitutions are sometimes difficult to see, the problem is usually easy to solve once you've got the substitution. 6.4.1

Substitutions

In substitution problems, an expression, like 3*, and the square of the expression, like 32x, appear in the same equation. Substituting another variable for the expression, like y = 3*, makes a quadratic in the new variable. We solve for the new variable (y), then find the original unknown (x) from our definition for the new one.
