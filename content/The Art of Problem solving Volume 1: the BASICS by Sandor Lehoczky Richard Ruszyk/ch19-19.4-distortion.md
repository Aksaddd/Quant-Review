Distortion

Draw a picture with a marker on a piece of cellophane. Now pull on the cellophane from either side, stretching the picture. You'll notice that while the figure gets wider, it does not get shorter or taller. This is a distortion. In a distortion, one dimension of a figure is multiplied by some factor, while the other dimension(s) remain the same. Distortions are equivalent to squishing or stretching figures. As an example of distortion, consider isosceles triangle ABC. By distorting the vertical scale of the diagram (i.e. by squishing down or stretching up), we can make the triangle taller or shorter. Thus, we can distort AABC into AABC' or AABC". Most notably, there is some distortion that maps the isosceles triangle into an equilateral triangle.
