## Overview

This chapter explores five general principles that are extremely helpful in the interview process. These guidelines — drawn from experience on both sides of the interview table — will better prepare you for quantitative finance job interviews and improve your likelihood of success as a candidate.

---

## Principle 1: Build a Broad Knowledge Base

The length and style of quant interviews differ from firm to firm. Landing a quant job may mean enduring hours of bombardment with brain teasers, calculus, linear algebra, probability theory, statistics, derivative pricing, or programming problems. To be a successful candidate, you need to have **broad knowledge in mathematics, finance, and programming**.

Will all these topics be relevant for your future quant job? Probably not. Each specific quant position often requires only limited knowledge in these domains. General problem solving skills may make more difference than specific knowledge.

### Why Are Quant Interviews So Comprehensive?

There are at least two reasons:

1. **Diverse interviewer backgrounds.** Each interviewer has his or her own favorite topics that are often related to their own educational background or work experience. As a result, the topics you will be tested on are likely to be very broad.

2. **Problem-solving skills correlate with knowledge breadth.** A basic understanding of a broad range of topics often helps you better analyze problems, explore alternative approaches, and come up with efficient solutions. Besides, your responsibility may not be restricted to your own projects — you are expected to contribute as a member of a bigger team.

> **Key Insight:** The keyword is *"basic understanding."* Interviewers do not expect you to be an expert on a specific subject — unless it happens to be your PhD thesis. The knowledge used in interviews, although broad, covers mainly essential concepts. **Know the basics very well.**

---

## Principle 2: Practice Your Interview Skills

The interview process starts long before you step into an interview room. In a sense, the success or failure of your interview is often determined before the first question is asked.

Your solutions to interview problems may fail to reflect your true intelligence and knowledge if you are unprepared. Although a complete review of quant interview problems is impossible and unnecessary, practice does improve your interview skills.

Furthermore, many of the behavioral, technical and resume-related questions can be anticipated.

> **Action Item:** Prepare yourself for potential questions long before you enter an interview room.

---

## Principle 3: Listen Carefully

You should be an **active listener** in interviews so that you understand the problems well before you attempt to answer them.

- If any aspect of a problem is not clear, politely ask for clarification.
- If the problem is more than a couple of sentences, jot down key words to help you remember all the information.
- For complex problems, interviewers often give away clues when they explain the problem.
- Even the assumptions they give may include information as to how to approach the problem.

> **Key Insight:** Listen carefully and make sure you get the necessary information before starting your solution.

---

## Principle 4: Speak Your Mind

When you analyze a problem and explore different ways to solve it, **never do it silently**.

- Clearly demonstrate your analysis and write down the important steps involved if necessary.
- This conveys your intelligence to the interviewer and shows that you are methodical and thorough.
- In case you go astray, the interaction will also give your interviewer the opportunity to correct course and provide hints.

Speaking your mind does **not** mean explaining every tiny detail. If some conclusions are obvious to you, simply state the conclusion without trivial details.

> **Key Insight:** More often than not, the interviewer uses a problem to test a specific concept or approach. Focus on demonstrating your understanding of the key concept rather than dwelling on less relevant details.

---

## Principle 5: Make Reasonable Assumptions

In real job settings, you are unlikely to have all the necessary information or data before you build a model and make a decision. In interviews, interviewers may not give you all the necessary assumptions either.

So it is up to you to make reasonable assumptions. The keyword here is **reasonable**.

- Explain your assumptions to the interviewer so that you will get immediate feedback.
- To solve quantitative problems, it is crucial that you can quickly make reasonable assumptions and design appropriate frameworks based on those assumptions.
